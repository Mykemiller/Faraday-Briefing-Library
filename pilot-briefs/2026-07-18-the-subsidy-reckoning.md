# The Subsidy Reckoning

*How the 2026 state backlash rewrote the terms of the data-center deal — and why the next siting cycle belongs to whoever underwrites their own power.*

## Slide 1 — Hypothesis *(preview)*

For a decade the American data center was sited on a single, frictionless bargain:
a state waived its sales tax, a hyperscaler broke ground, and the grid bill went to
everyone else. In 2026 that bargain broke — not at the margin, but at its
foundation. The costs finally became visible, and once visible they became
political. Georgia's data-center tax exemption came in **664% over projection at
$2.5 billion**; Ohio's ran **twelve times its forecast**. Nine states have already
moved from complaint to statute — pauses, moratoriums, phase-outs — and community
opposition blocked or delayed a documented **$98 billion** of projects between March
and June alone.

**Faraday's read:** this is not a wall. It is a repricing. The public subsidy that
underwrote US expansion is being replaced by a cost-recovery regime, and the
operators who internalize that first will inherit the constrained markets.

## Slide 2 — The costs became visible *(preview)*

The trigger was arithmetic, not activism. Good Jobs First — whose Subsidy Tracker
logs **251 subsidized data-center deals across 16 states since 2020**, led by
Indiana's **$8.2 billion** package for Amazon — spent the spring putting a number on
the giveaway, and the numbers did the rest:

- **Ohio** surrendered **$1.6 billion** to its exemption in 2025, roughly **12× the
  original forecast**, and Governor DeWine paused new requests.
- **Georgia** revised its FY2026 cost projection **upward by 664%, to $2.5 billion**.
- The recurring drain runs into the billions a year — Good Jobs First's *Cloudy with
  a Loss of Spending Control* puts Georgia at **$2.5B annually**, Virginia at
  **$1.94B**, and Texas at **$1B** — with states recovering, on the programs
  measured, **between 9 and 48 cents on the dollar**.
- Transparency is worse than the cost: of at least **36 states** with data-center
  subsidies, only **11 disclose which companies receive them**.

When a line item is invisible it is permanent. Once it prints at billions, it is a
target.

## Slide 3 — Nine states moved from talk to statute

The pullback is legislative, bipartisan, and fast:

- **Arizona** enacted a **three-year moratorium** on its data-center sales-tax
  exemption (HB 4168 / SB 1861, signed by Gov. Hobbs), running **1 Jul 2026 – 30 Jun
  2029**.
- **Illinois** halted new applications to its incentive program as of **1 Jul 2026**
  after the legislature adjourned without the reforms Gov. Pritzker sought.
- **New York's** legislature passed a **one-year moratorium** on permits for loads
  ≥20 MW (S10642 / A11560 — Senate 44–16, Assembly 102–39), sent to Gov. Hochul on
  **5 Jun 2026**; her signature would make New York the **first statewide freeze in
  the country**.
- **Florida** went the other direction to the same end: Gov. DeSantis signed **SB
  484**, forcing data centers to **cover their own utility and infrastructure costs**
  and handing land-use authority back to local governments.
- **Oklahoma** passed a **Data Center Customer Ratepayer Protection Act**, requiring
  large-load tariffs so operators reimburse utilities for the capacity they draw.

Behind the statehouses, the localities moved faster still: **Seattle** passed an
**emergency moratorium** (CB 121214, 9 Jun 2026); at least **14 states** now carry
active pauses or proposals.

## Slide 4 — The grid stopped socializing the bill

The most durable change isn't the moratoria — those expire. It's the tariff. As of
May 2026, **23 states have approved at least one large-load tariff**, with **77 more
pending across 36 states** (Columbia Law's Sabin Center). These rewrite who pays for
growth:

- **AEP Ohio's** tariff binds customers ≥25 MW to **85% capacity payments** on
  **12-year** minimum terms — after which AEP's large-load forecast **fell by half**.
- **Dominion's** GS-5 tariff (Virginia) auto-enrolls 25 MW+ loads from **1 Jan
  2027**: **14-year** terms and **$1.5 million per MW** in collateral.

Washington is moving the same direction. FERC's **Docket RM26-4-000** (18 Jun 2026)
pushes interconnection cost-causation **onto the requesting load** rather than the
ratepayer base, and in March seven leading AI companies signed a **Ratepayer
Protection Pledge** committing to build or buy the generation their campuses consume.
Even the federal permitting accelerant — **Executive Order 14318** — is aimed at
projects that bring **>100 MW of new electricity** and **$500M+** of their own capital.

The through-line: the cost of the grid is being moved from the public ledger to the
operator's balance sheet.

## Slide 5 — And the community became a counterparty

Where fiscal policy set the price, local opposition set the risk. Heatmap Pro counted
**20+ projects canceled after local pushback in Q1 2026** — **$41.7 billion** and
**3.5 GW** — and **53 active opposition groups across 17 states** contesting 30
projects in Q2. The signature loss: Blackstone/QTS's **2,100-acre Prince William
"Digital Gateway"**, the largest campus ever planned, **killed when the Virginia
Court of Appeals voided its rezoning on a notice defect** (31 Mar 2026).

The industry's answer is to make the community a signatory. The **Community Benefits
Agreement** is now standard in contested markets:

- **Lancaster, Pennsylvania** produced the first data-center-specific CBA —
  **$20.25M** in contributions, a **20,000-gallon/day water cap** per campus, and a
  **100% clean-energy** requirement over 20 years.
- **El Paso** voted **unanimously** (23 Jun 2026) to negotiate a legally enforceable
  CBA with **Meta**, with a 300-job "El Paso First" commitment.
- **Michigan** would make the practice law, mandating CBAs before permits (HB 6137 /
  SB 1050).

## Faraday's Take *(not shown in preview)*

The decade of the free data center is over, and it is not coming back. What replaced
it is not prohibition — it is **underwriting**.

Read the three instruments together — the tariff, the ratepayer pledge, and the CBA
— and they resolve to one demand: *bring your own power, and pay for your own
externalities.* That is a cost-of-capital event. The all-in cost of a US megacampus
just rose by the price of long-term firm generation, hard collateral, and a
20-year community obligation — and it rose most where the grid is tightest.

Here is the move the market hasn't priced in yet. The reflexive fear inside the
industry is the "closed for business" signal — the Data Center Coalition's phrase for
what a moratorium tells capital. That reading is a decade too old. Moratoria are
**temporary**; tariffs and CBAs are **structural**. The binding constraint was never
the tax break — Texas already forgoes a billion a year in incentives and still can't
add generation fast enough, with ERCOT's large-load queue up nearly **300%** in a
year. The binding constraint is **deliverable power on terms the public will
tolerate**. Subsidy was never the moat. Power is.

So the winners of the next siting cycle are not the operators hunting the deepest
exemption in the friendliest state. They are the ones who can walk into a hostile
county with their own generation contracted, a ratepayer-neutral tariff already
signed, and a CBA drafted before the first hearing — and clear a permit that survives
appeal. That capability consolidates. The hyperscalers and colo operators who build
it become the only counterparties a constrained jurisdiction can say yes to. **The
backlash won't stop the buildout. It will decide who gets to do it.**

*— Faraday's read is that "closed for business" is a misread of a repricing. We
think the more likely path is consolidation toward the operators who can
self-underwrite power. Watch the large-load-tariff count and the CBA-before-permit
pattern; those are the leading indicators, not the moratorium headlines.*

**Conflict resolution notes (zero-fabrication discipline):**

1. **The 664% / $2.5B statistic.** A downstream wire (dmnews.com) attributed the
   "$2.5B, 664% over a $327M estimate" figure to *Illinois*. Good Jobs First — the
   originating analysis — attributes it to *Georgia's* FY2026 projection. This brief
   uses **Georgia**, per the primary source, and treats Illinois only as a
   *program-halt* event (DCEO / Pritzker), which is independently documented. The
   more dramatic mis-attribution was not adopted.
2. **Virginia's annual cost** appears as both ~$1.02B (FY2024) and $1.94B (annual)
   across reports using different fiscal bases. The brief cites the single *Cloudy
   with a Loss of Spending Control* figure ($1.94B) to keep one internally
   consistent report as the basis rather than blending vintages.
3. **Maine / "first statewide moratorium."** Crawled sources conflict on Maine's
   status (a passed moratorium vs. a gubernatorial veto). Rather than assert a
   contested sequence, the brief anchors the "first statewide freeze" claim on **New
   York**, where the vote counts and transmittal date are cleanly documented, and
   omits Maine's disputed specifics.
